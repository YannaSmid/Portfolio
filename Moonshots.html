Moonshots

Playing a space video game while you are actually in space!

Nikki Rademaker, and Yanna Smid

Project Overview

As part of MOONSHOTS ‘24, a national innovation challenge organized by the Netherlands Space Office (NSO) and astronaut André Kuipers, our team developed a game concept to support astronaut mental health aboard the ISS. After months of ideation, research, and feedback from real astronauts, we created a space shooter prototype designed to provide relaxation during long, high-stress missions in space.

The main goal for this project is to come up with an innovative idea that can be useful to NASA. We proposed our Space Shooting Video Game, that uses the actual space environment as the in-game environment. Astronauts can play this video game in the Capula, to relax and to have some fun, while enjoying the sci-fi reality.

Features

  • Mental Health Focus: Based on psychological research into astronaut well-being and the therapeutic potential of video games.
  • Unique Space Integration: This video game cannot be played on earth, because it uses the real view from the Cupola observatory on the ISS as a visual backdrop. It is purely meant for astronauts in space!
  • AR Game Concept: Designed to run on AR glasses already used aboard the ISS, eliminating the need for hardware changes.
  • Custom Controls: Tested several input methods (eye tracking, gestures, joystick), ultimately proposing a simple arcade-style controller optimized for microgravity.
  • Friendly Competition: Includes a scoring system and plans for an in-mission leaderboard to boost morale and social bonding.

Technical Details

  • Programming: Unity, C#
  • Modeling: Blender for the astroids modeling and animation
  • User tests were conducted to test the prototype
  • Presentation: Our idea was presented at the MOONSHOTS'24 conference, to real astronauts.

Reflection

This project brought together technical development, speculative design, and mental health research. Working on AstroArcade showed how game design can extend beyond entertainment and be used for the well-being of astronauts. Collaborating with astronauts inspired us to account for real-world constraints like microgravity and spatial limitations. It also sharpened our ability to explain complex technical designs to non-technical experts.
