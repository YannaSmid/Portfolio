FlightWave

Wave to your next flight, with this interactive information system.

By Yanna Smid

Project Overview

Airports are places of anticipation, of reunions, adventures, and new beginnings. But for many, they’re also filled with stress, uncertainty, and long hours of waiting. We asked: What if looking up your flight could feel less like decoding a spreadsheet, and more like exploring the world with your hands?

This project for Human Computer Interaction, reimagines flight information displays as an interactive, 3D globe interface, controlled entirely by hand gestures using Leap Motion. Instead of static screens filled with hard-to-read text, passengers can now physically engage with a dynamic world map. With the interactive FlightWave, you can see exactly where your flight will go and can interact with it in a way that’s playful, intuitive, and calming. Whether you’re a nervous traveler who needs reassurance, or a curious one excited by the journey ahead—this system makes the wait more meaningful. It transforms flight anxiety into wonder, and passive waiting into active exploration.

Features

  • 3D Flight Route Visualization: Users can see the actual path their plane will take across a rotating globe.
  • Touchless Interaction: Powered by Leap Motion, passengers control the system using only hand gestures—no screens to touch, improving hygiene and accessibility.
  • Custom Gesture System: Swipe to rotate the globe, Point to select a destination, Palm-up gesture to stop movement
  • Custom Controls: Tested several input methods (eye tracking, gestures, joystick), ultimately proposing a simple arcade-style controller optimized for microgravity.
  • Menu & Instruction Screens: Help new users quickly understand the gestures with interactive demos.

Technical Details

  • Programming: Unity, C#
  • Leap Motion SDK: Used for capturing and translating hand gestures
  • User tests were conducted to test the prototype
  • Custom Gesture Mapping: Designed to reflect familiar, real-world movements such as rotating a physical globe or raising a hand to stop something

Reflection

The project started with a simple question: how can we make airport monitors more informative, fun, and stress-relieving? Many passengers experience flight anxiety—especially when unsure about where their plane is flying. By visualizing the flight path, this tool can offer both reassurance and curiosity. The development taught us how to design around real-world user experience constraints, from motion control limitations to age inclusivity and instruction clarity. Creating a fun, intuitive, and hygienic system also showed how small design tweaks can make technology feel more human and less intimidating. Ultimately, this project showcased how emerging tech like Leap Motion can go beyond novelty and support meaningful improvements in everyday environments like airports.
